# Chapter 1: Idea Validation

You have a SaaS idea. Before you write a single line of code, you need to know if anyone will pay for it.

Most founders skip this step. They spend months building, launch to silence, and wonder what went wrong. The answer is almost always the same: they built something nobody asked for.

Validation doesn't need to take months. You can get a clear signal in a week.

## The Three Questions

Every SaaS idea needs to pass three tests:

1. **Does this problem exist?** Are real people actively trying to solve this problem right now?
2. **Are they paying to solve it?** Existing spend (on competing tools, freelancers, or manual workarounds) proves willingness to pay.
3. **Can you reach them?** A great product for an audience you can't find is a hobby, not a business.

## Step 1: Find Evidence of the Problem

Don't ask friends if your idea is good. They'll say yes to be nice. Instead, look for evidence:

- **Search Reddit** for people describing the problem. Check r/SaaS, r/startups, r/Entrepreneur, and niche subreddits for your industry. Look for posts with high engagement where people ask "Is there a tool that does X?"
- **Search X/Twitter** for complaints about existing solutions. People publicly vent about tools that frustrate them. That frustration is your opportunity.
- **Check G2 and Capterra** reviews of competitors. One-star and two-star reviews tell you exactly what's broken in your market.
- **Google the problem** with phrases like "best tool for [X]" or "[competitor] alternative." If people are searching, they're spending.

## Step 2: Validate Willingness to Pay

Free users are not validation. You need evidence of spending.

- **Competitor pricing pages** tell you the price range the market accepts. If three competitors charge $29 to $99 per month, you know the ballpark.
- **Job postings** for the role your tool replaces show how much companies spend on the manual version.
- **Freelancer rates** on Upwork for the task your tool automates prove the budget exists.

If nobody is currently spending money to solve this problem, they probably won't start spending on your solution either.

## Step 3: Talk to Five People

Five conversations with potential customers will teach you more than six months of market research. The key: don't pitch your solution. Ask about their problem.

Good questions:

- "How are you handling [problem] today?"
- "What have you tried? What didn't work?"
- "How much time/money does this cost you per month?"
- "If a tool solved this perfectly, what would that look like?"

Bad questions:

- "Would you use an app that does X?" (Everyone says yes. It means nothing.)
- "How much would you pay for this?" (People are terrible at predicting future spending.)

Read *The Mom Test* by Rob Fitzpatrick for the full framework. It's the single best book on customer validation.

## Step 4: Build a Landing Page, Not a Product

Before you build anything, put up a one-page site that describes your product and includes a signup form or waitlist. Drive traffic to it from the communities where you found evidence of the problem.

Track two numbers:

- **Visitor-to-signup rate**: Above 10% is a strong signal. Below 3% means your positioning needs work.
- **Signup volume**: Can you get 50 signups in a week? That's your distribution test.

## When to Move Forward

You've validated enough when you can answer yes to all three:

- You found at least 10 people actively complaining about this problem online
- Competitors or manual workarounds cost $20+/month (proving willingness to pay)
- You got 50+ signups on a landing page or 5+ people who said "I'd pay for that" in a conversation
