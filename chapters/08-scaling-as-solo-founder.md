# Chapter 8: Scaling as a Solo Founder

You launched. People are paying. Now you need to grow without burning out or going broke.

Scaling as a solo founder is a different game than scaling with a team. You don't have a VP of Engineering, a DevOps person, or a customer success manager. You have yourself, your tools, and 24 hours in a day.

The founders who succeed at this stage don't work harder. They work on fewer things and automate everything else.

## The Three Phases of Solo Scaling

### Phase 1: $0 to $1,000 MRR (Do Everything Manually)

At this stage, don't automate anything. Do it all by hand.

- Reply to every support email personally. You'll learn more about your product in one week of support than in a month of analytics.
- Onboard users manually. Get on a call. Watch them use your product. Note where they get confused.
- Ship features based on what users tell you, not what you think they need.

This phase is ugly and unscalable. That's the point. You're building the knowledge base you'll need to automate later.

### Phase 2: $1,000 to $5,000 MRR (Automate the Repetitive)

Now you start replacing yourself:

- **Support**: Create a knowledge base or FAQ page. Most questions are the same 10 questions repeated. Write answers once, link to them forever.
- **Onboarding**: Build an in-app onboarding flow that replaces your manual calls. Tooltips, checklists, and welcome emails handle the first 80%.
- **Billing**: Stripe's customer portal lets users manage their own subscriptions. No more manual cancellations and plan changes.
- **Monitoring**: Set up alerts so you only look at dashboards when something breaks, not every morning.

### Phase 3: $5,000+ MRR (Multiply Your Output)

This is where AI tools become essential, not optional.

- Use AI agents to build features in hours instead of days. A feature that takes you 20 hours of manual coding can be shipped in under an hour with the right AI build system.
- Use AI for code review, testing, and security scanning. These are the tasks that slow solo founders down the most because they require context-switching.
- Use AI for content and marketing. Repurpose one piece of content across LinkedIn, X, Instagram, and Reddit without writing each version from scratch.

## What to Outsource (and What to Keep)

### Keep In-House

- **Product decisions**: What to build next, which features to kill, how to price. Nobody knows your users better than you.
- **Customer conversations**: Even when you have a knowledge base, personally respond to complex or emotional support requests. It builds loyalty that no chatbot can replicate.
- **Positioning and messaging**: How you describe your product determines who buys it. This is founder work.

### Outsource or Automate

- **Repetitive development work**: Infrastructure, boilerplate, CRUD operations. AI handles this well.
- **Security audits**: Automated scanning catches 90% of vulnerabilities.
- **Performance optimization**: Tools can identify slow queries and large bundles without human analysis.
- **Design execution**: Once you've defined your brand, a designer or design system can handle individual screens and components.
- **Bookkeeping and taxes**: Use Bench, Pilot, or a local accountant. Your time is worth more than the fee.

## The Biggest Risk: You

The single biggest risk to a solo SaaS business is founder burnout.

Signs you're heading there:

- You dread opening your laptop
- You're building features nobody asked for because customer conversations feel exhausting
- You haven't taken a day off in 3+ weeks
- You're reactive to every support ticket instead of proactive about the product

The fix: reduce scope. Say no to feature requests that don't serve your core use case. Automate the tasks that drain you. Take weekends off. A sustainable 20-hour week beats an unsustainable 60-hour week every time.
